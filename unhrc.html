Topic 1: electoral Violence

     Electoral violence is defined the random or organized act to intimidate, physically harm, blackmail, or abuse a political stakeholder in seeking to determine, delay, or otherwise influence an electoral process. The United Nations has made it their obligation to provide free and fair elections, as made apparent in the Universal Declaration of Human Rights (UDHR). Ensuring and protecting the human right to frequent and authentic elections requires the abolition of electoral violence in areas that are politically unstable.
     Relatively new democracies share a long history rising from one-party and dominant political parties where politics and economies are combined. Furthermore, socio-economic and political advantages of democracies have yet to be understood within these countries. Usually, differing structural conditions during high stakes electoral process create high incentives for violence. Electoral violence targets four main populations: 1) electoral stakeholders (voters, candidates); 2) electoral information (registration data); 3) electoral facilities (polling); 4) electoral events (campaign rallies). Hence, part of protecting human rights involves the identification of motives behind violent acts, predicting times and identifying the actors involved.
     As delegates of UNHRC, it is your responsibility identify areas prone to electoral violence along with identifiers and signals to recognize. It is the delegates task to outline comprehensive plans that focus on mitigating electoral violence in the areas where it is prevalent as well as promote free and fair elections as the new norm.


Topic 2: Protecting Minorities

     With a rapidly globalizing world, the protecting of minorities and their rights has become a priority. In many countries where this has been a long-standing problem, sufficient media coverage has been provided, whereas in countries where this is a new phenomenon, proper integration is imperative for those minorities to be fully accepted into society. In a consensus adopted by the United Nations, it states that minorities are based on ethical, cultural, religious and linguistic identities, and provides that their respective states should provide them with refuge and protection.
     Contrary to expectation, this is not the case be it in the East or the West. The foundations of human rights rests on the fact that they apply to every individual equally. In the case of ethnic minorities these codes of ethics are completely ignored, Muslims in Rome and Italy face persistent hostility and discrimination. In addition steps must be taken to protect Australian Aboriginals and South American Native tribes. Minorities in all regions face blatant and direct discrimination in their daily lives.
     As delegates of the UNHRC, it is your responsibility to determine how such ingrained discrimination can be turned around. The delegates should focus on how to integrate minorities into the social and political lives of their respective countries as well as outline policies that can be implemented in the global context to combat this issue.
